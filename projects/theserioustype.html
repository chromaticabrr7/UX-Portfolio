CASE STUDY
The Serious Type
A mobile app built to provide a productive, community-based learning space for people of all ages. The Serious Type app shifts its focus away from the traditional social media engagement model and works to help users make decisions driven by intention and the desire to share and grow as a collective.
UX Design Apprentice
December - February 2022
My Role
I was onboarded during the second week of launch to round out a team of 14 researchers, designers, and strategists. During research I played a key role in interviewing potential users during the research phase of the project. During the design phase I brought many of the team’s individual concepts to the table and unified them under a single, component-driven, design language that guided much of the ideation and prototyping process.
The Challenge
Challenging the Social Media Norm

At the outset of the project the client’s challenge was relatively straightforward: turn their existing desktop platform into a mobile application that can inspire engagement and remedy user retention issues. Early discussions with the client, however, revealed an opportunity to challenge ourselves further and ask, what can we do to take on the existing social media model and turn it into a creatively productive platform?
A Productive Space for the Next Generation
The Serious Type is a 501c3 non-profit organization, based out of Colorado, for youth that are “serious about their lives and future”. Through their online platform they help young people develop interests in matters of the world, setting them up to contribute and express themselves creatively via mediums such as writing, photography, discussion, and more.
The Approach
Leave No Stone Unturned
With new direction, we quickly realized the need for extensive research into relevant areas before making any sort of design decision. An allotment of 10-15 hours per week of working time meant sprints needed to be organized meticulously and executed flexibly.
Agile Sprints Grounded in Research
In order to pursue every possible direction without steering too far off course, we decided to explore, prioritize, and organize our high level curiosities each week before bringing our findings to the client during end-of-week demos. The discussion guided our direction for the following sprint.
[fig 1] Eight week sprint structure
Following the Thread
As with any project, there were more questions than answers. To ensure a thorough yet manageable research plan, our team listed multiple assumptions and considered their research and project implications. Threads of significance were built into survey and user interview plans to glean quantitative and qualitative information.
[fig 2] Sample list of assumptions
Love the Problem, Not the Solution
This mantra played a big part in keeping the transition from research to design focused on the big picture. Having performed detailed analyses during the primary research phase, ensuring a quick design turnaround came down to ideating on the macro and focusing on the micro later.
Keeping Stakeholders in the Loop, Building Rapport
Building and maintaining a consistent relationship with the client allowed our team to lead more exploratory research and opened the door to conversations that informed not only the project at hand but also the client’s brand as a whole. Feedback was more honest and the design process was afforded more flexibility.
The Discovery
How Can Social Media Be More Constructive?
Our four weeks of research into social media use and trends were made up of a few key areas of exploration. Competitive analysis gave us an overview of the social media landscape from a business perspective. A quick audit of the desktop platform revealed The Serious Type’s information architecture. Academic exploration addressed our ongoing list of assumptions and guided the structure of user surveys and interviews. Data collected from over 100 social media users showed:

Have used social media for over four years
40%
Use social media for activism
68%
Use social media several times per day
72%
Use social media to learn new skills

Pivoting From Some Users to All Users
Early exploration into mentorship and platform moderation got the team thinking about which age groups we would most likely cater to. After multiple discussions, we decided to pursue all age groups while maintaining an emphasis on young adults. This kept the door open to older users who might bring mentorship qualities to the platform while young adults remained the focus.
[fig 4] Pivot Emphasis
The Vision
Powered by Purpose
The goal was to build a platform that would facilitate decision-making backed by the drive to learn and act. The Serious Type was going to move as far away from the passive social media consumption model as possible. We wanted to promote a different social media model —consumption powered by purpose.
Fostering Collaboration and Creativity
We found learning as a collective to be a powerful form of expression and growth that the platform needed to support in some form. Structuring a space that would allow for this was key to building out the app.
Paving the Way for Mentorship
Learning requires leadership. With much of the user pool looking to find mentors in their areas of interest, we wanted to build beyond the status quo and explore a more productive mentor-mentee relationship.
Encouraging Action
In a digital world where quickly digestible content and short attention spans are driving most, if not all social media platform models, we wanted to slow things down and encourage users to do something with what they’ve learned. A network of dreamers turns information into action.
The Requirements
Designing Outside of the Social Media Mold
During the transition from research to design it was obvious the road that lay ahead was not going to be easy to navigate. From a business perspective the platform needed more users and for them to stay. The ethical stipulations set up by the client addressed social media patterns and tactics we needed to avoid.
Shedding Light on Social Media Dark Patterns
From day one the client made one thing clear: social media features (likes, comments, etc) that fed insecurities were not going to be supported by the app. Building a space for users to feel comfortable sharing and discussing the work they created was of utmost importance.
Encouraging Mindful Interaction
Our hope was that every second spent on the app would exercise the conscious mind in some way. While most platforms on the market didn’t do much more than encourage endlessly scrolling and disengaging from the real world, we made a point of facilitating the opposite.
The Framework
A Component-Based Approach
Early sketches by members of the design team all pointed towards users interacting with bundles of information, whether they be a posted photo alongside a caption, a written article, or a live session being held sometime in the future. Knowing the app was going to be structured around these pieces of information, I suggested packaging them into a component that was variable and could be customized easily.
[fig 5] Component Sketch to Design Process
Building Guardrails
Once the information components had been established, the remaining architecture could be built around housing and interacting with those components. To account for MVP requirements established by the strategy team, user flows were built to provide reference and ensure all necessary interactions were being incorporated in ideation and prototyping.

Detailed Design
The Serious Type Mobile App
A collaborative effort over the course of eight weeks led to the reimagining of a platform that hopes to make space for creative energy, foster community, and reinvigorate the next generation.
[fig 7] Blown up overview of app.
Promote Discovery and Exploration
Rooms are compact communities built to help users find and explore new interests, meet like-minded people, learn new skills, and be mentored. Users can join as many rooms as they like and contribute however they feel. The space is designed to be navigated with ease via highly visible cards and dynamic content placement.
[fig 8] Discovering rooms
Collaboration Whenever Possible
It’s easier to engage and learn when the people you’re with can communicate in real-time. Sessions provide spaces within Rooms to interact with peers and mentors by providing the ability to collaborate through video, audio, and text. Mentors can lead sessions on a daily or weekly basis while users can create private sessions to engage within smaller groups.
[fig 9]Entering a session
Scrolling, Not for the Sake of Scrolling
The home feed needed to be simple and concise. Users can get a high level glimpse of the content populating their areas of interest while circumventing the infinite scroll that so often contributes to mindless engagement. Less opportunity for scrolling means more time for doing.
[fig 10] Home Feed tabs
Pitching a Productive, Purposeful Alternative
Helping the user understand the vision behind the app was important in getting everyone on the same page. A short but powerful onboarding process was the perfect venue for this reminder.
[fig 11] Onboarding copy
The Impact
Something to Show
A platform that had a simple website and wealthy vision but struggled with retention now has a mobile concept to back that vision. As funding is one of the client’s primary concerns in establishing longevity, this project has provided them with a proof-of-concept to showcase amongst potential donors. Funding opens the door to development and eventually, a marketable product.
Looking Forward
Phase Two
This eight week project was always structured around researching and building an experimental concept that could possibly be brought into development given it was approved by the client. There was never any certainty that the team would be moving forward. That being said, the deliverables have garnered a lot of support on the stakeholder side and it is expected that the project will be moving into its second phase in short order.
Validating What We've Got
Given the time constraints there was little opportunity for the most recent iteration of our prototype to be tested amongst users. To kick off research and design efforts in the next phase it is expected that a test plan will be put together to acquire a complete set of qualitative information. An analysis will be performed and personas will be referenced accordingly to determine how we can better satisfy our core user needs.
What is Meaningful Mentorship?
Again, due to time, the concept of mentorship was left only partially addressed in the app’s design. This was also partly due to a lack of qualitative information on mentor-mentee relationships. Exploration in this area is necessary and will be made a high priority item during phase two.
